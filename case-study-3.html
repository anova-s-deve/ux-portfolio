    About 98 million people live in distressing sanitary conditions across urban slums in India. The lack of household toilets pose not just health risks but also safety risks to women and other vulnerable demographics like the disabled, the elderly and the ill. There have been countless reported cases of women and those vulnerable being harassed on their way to community toilets or while defecating in the open, away from their homes, especially post sundown. The problem of sanitation in urban slums has plagued generations of urban planners. There is no universal model that works across cultures to solve this problem. Here we introduce you to one that seems to be gaining momentum in the state of Maharashtra through years of patient research, experimentation and evolution.

    Introducing the OHOT Model

    Recognizing the problems posed by open defecation as well as community toilets that have their own set of disadvantages; Shelter Associates (SA), a Pune based grassroots level non-profit has over the years perfected a safe and affordable solution to the sanitation problem that focuses on facilitating individual household toilets through its data driven, replicable, scalable and multi-stakeholder inclusive ‚ÄòOne Home One Toilet‚Äô (OHOT) model . The Initiative was awarded The Local Project Challenge Honor Award 2020 at The World Urban Forum by the UN Habitat.

    The model involves construction materials facilitated by our donors being delivered at the beneficiaries' doorstep free of cost. The beneficiaries then contribute towards the construction of the toilet instilling a sense of ownership.

    My Role

    Problems identified

    On conducting usability tests of the existing user flows with the Executive Director, M&E Manager, Project Manager and GIS analysts, these broad problems were identified:

    1. Inconsistent raw data : Lack of a consistent methodology in questionnaires used over time rendered 20 years' worth of data on sanitation of informal settlements across these 5 districts in different formats, unfit for integration and analysis.
    2. Varying map design : Maps made by different analysts followed different cartographic styles due to the lack of a cartographic style guide. An outdated proprietary GIS software, also lacking in creating map templates, was hindering the team's scalability due to limited number of licenses.
    3. Dashboards without insights : The dashboard designed did not provide any insights from the data collected by SA. The dashboard was not designed for any user in specific and hence was not the user's first choice for insights.

    Red Routes

    Based on the problems identified and requirements collected from the users, the following red routes were proposed:

    1. Survey form redesign and data migration
    2. Cartographic style guide and themes
    3. Dashboard redesign

    1. Survey form redesign and Data migration

    We switched to an open-source web-based application called Kobo Toolbox for defining and deploying survey forms and it's android app which also works offline for data collection. Existing raw data was evaluated and cleaned. Surveys were redesigned to incorporate form logic that includes validating and restricting responses and types, conditional questions, setting defaults and cascading lists.

    The data collection was segregated into the following surveys:

    1. Rapid Infrastructure Mapping ‚Äì mapping of existing infrastructure of slum
    2. Rapid Household Surveys ‚Äì collecting household level data
    3. Individual factsheet ‚Äì for maintaining record of completed toilets

    The process involved charting elaborate decision trees for each of the surveys and defining them on Kobo Form Builder as per the logic developed with user research. Click on the image below to view the decision tree prepared for the Rapid Household Survey.

    All the existing data in multiple formats was migrated to the newly created forms. This migrated data then fed into the dashboards designed for different users.

    2. Cartographic style guides and themes

    Based on usability studies of existing maps with GIS Analysts, the M&E manager and the Executive Director interacting with maps in ways ranging from its preparation to presentations, it was observed that developing a cartographic style guide and correcting the use of colour across maps was required.

    25 thematic maps were shortlisted to be prepared by Tanaji The GIS analyst. Using design best practices, the maps were categorised into 3 colour schemes for spatial data visualisation. The basic principle for the colour schemes is that variations in hue denote nominal/categorical differences while variations in lightness show ordinal differences.

    We take the example of thematic maps of Kalakhadak in Kolhapur to demonstrate the visualisation style guide.

    1. Qualitative scheme

      2. Nominal data like the 'Type of structure occupancy', 'Ownership status of the house', 'Type of water connection', 'Facility of household solid waste collection', 'Current place of defecation', 'Type of toilet preferrred' etc. use a qualitative color scheme to illuminate each of the themes and spatial patterns if any.

      This is a map that shows the type of structure occupancy in Kalakhadak, Kolhapur. This map uses variations in hue to highlight each nominal category to the user.

    2. Sequential scheme

      3. Ordinal data such as 'Type of structure of the house', 'House area in sq.ft.', 'Proximity to sewage connection' are represented best by sequential color schemes.

      The map below shows the house area in sq.ft. across Kalakhadak. It uses gradations in lightness to convey the ordinal differences in the size of tenements in the settlement.

    3. Binary scheme

      4. Nominal differences with two opposing data points like a yes or no survey question served as the sample of binary color schemes. Data representing whether a 'household was or wasn't interested in having a household toilet built', the 'ownership status of the occupants' etc. are examples for this scheme.

      The map below shows the data for whether or not the household is interested in building a toilet. This map uses two contrasting hues to depict binary data .

    The map design was decided in consultation with the team based on their requirements. The map and data templates for the preparation of all 25 thematic maps was created on an open source GIS software called QGIS.

    This exercise made the maps prepared by Shelter unique and the process of preparation of the maps much easier and faster than before. It also contributed to the maps conveying necessary information clearly.

    3. Dashboard redesign

    This redesign project consists of 3 dashboards for Lata The Executive Director, Smita The M&E Manager and Dhanasree The Project Manager respectively. The dashboards are a part of the 'Shelter App', a platform for managing and maintaining the data collected by Shelter Associates. Sanitation and hygiene related data is of much value to various institutions for research and developmental activities. In addition to this data, there is also a generous amount of operational data that must be converted into insights for team managers.

    Goals for the dashboard

    1. Prime real estate on the dashboard must be dedicated to opeartional metrics.
    2. The dashboard must answer frequently asked questions at a glance.
    3. The dashboard must focus on personalisation for each user over the ability to customise.

    i. Dashboard for Lata The Executive Director

    The dashboards tab consists of 3 sub-tabs- 'Toilet construction', 'Community mobilisation' and 'RHS and Follow-up'. In this case study, we will be looking at the 'Toilet construction' dashboard.

    There are 3 levels of filters to the dashboard to view insights- 'All', 'City level', 'Slum level'. Lata has access to information at every level. However, her default view would be personalised at the level 'All'.

    The card titled 'Budget exhausted vs. Toilets constructed' highlighed the budget in red when it crossed a threshold of an excess of 5% in spending than the toilets completed. This was a threshold identified by the CEO and M&E Manager for raising an alarm to take action.

    ii. Dashboard for Smita The M&E Manager

    Smita also has access to information at every level. However, her default view would be personalised at the level of any one of the cities where the work is ongoing. She also has access to insights at the level 'All' but does not see the operational cards at that level.

    iii. Dashboard for Dhanasree The Project Manager

    Dhanasree's access is restricted to the city assigned to her, for example, Navi Mumbai and the slums within the city. Her default view is at the level of any one of the slums where the work is ongoing.

    Donor management using the dashboard enabled timely communication and resulted in better relationship with donors. The metrics identified also enabled management of funds and identification of excess spending. Overall, it resulted in reduced stress, increased visibility and proved to be a tremendous time saver for its users.
