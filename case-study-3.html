    Shelter App Redesign

    System and Product Redesign
    2017-2018

    About 54% of the people inhabiting the overcrowded phenomenon of urban slums India were plagued with distressing sanitary conditions in 2018. The lack of access to household toilets and subsequent open defecation posing environmental risks begins to bear an even uglier face in cases where the safety and health of women, the disabled, elderly or the ill are compromised.

    Introducing the OHOT Model

    Recognizing the problems posed by open defecation as well as community toilets that have their own set of disadvantages; Shelter Associates (SA), a Pune based grassroots level non-profit has over the years perfected a safe and affordable solution to the sanitation problem that focuses on facilitating individual household toilets through its data driven, replicable, scalable and multi-stakeholder inclusive ‘One Home One Toilet’ (OHOT) model . The Initiative was awarded The Local Project Challenge Honor Award 2020 at The World Urban Forum by the UN Habitat.

    The model involves the construction materials facilitated by our donors being delivered at the beneficiaries' doorstep free of cost for them and the beneficiary family contributing towards the construction of the toilet instilling a sense of ownership among the beneficiaries.

    My Role

    My engagement with SA was piqued by my interest in mapping informal human settlements for their documentation and progression. I worked in the capacity of a designer redesigning the product called Shelter App and in the process also worked on some very interesting and niche spatial data management and visualisation.

    User Research

    Workflow

    SA has been involved in mapping settlement patterns and infrastructure in and around slums across Pune, Kolhapur, Sangli, Pimpri Chinchwad and recently Navi Mumbai partnering with local government bodies to deliver sanitation infrastructure to individual households in slums. The workflow is as follows:

    User Personas

    1. Executive Director
      "I need a summarised dashboard to stay up to date with our delivery and overall reach."

    2. Monitoring & Evaluation (M&E) Manager
      "I need to be able to assess the progress of work across cities on a day-to-day basis and manage all the data generated through mapping and surveys."

    3. Project Manager at city level
      "I need to be able to monitor progress in my city and assign work to community workers."

    4. GIS analyst
      "I need to be able to draw up required maps more efficiently and would want the process of data cleaning eradicated for every slum surveyed."

    5. Donor
      "I want to see where my money is being used."

    6. Engineers at Municipal Corporations
      "We need access to city-wide GIS data on sanitation in slums and to be able to monitor progress of delivery."

    Problems identified

    On conducting usability tests of the existing user flows with the Executive Director, M&E Manager, Project Manager and GIS analysts, these broad problems were identified:

    1. Due to different individuals handling data collection and other socio-political changes in the scenario being surveyed, 20 years' worth of data on sanitation of informal settlements across these 5 districts was in different formats, hence, unfit for integration and analysis.
    2. Using a proprietary GIS software which hasn't had any updates in the past 5 years was affecting the team's scalability and efficiency.
    3. Maps made by different analysts followed different cartographic styles due to the lack of a cartographic style guide.
    4. Dashboards designed were not optimised to be used by different stakeholders.

    Red Routes

    Based on the problems identified and requirements collected from the users, the following red routes were proposed:

    1. Survey form redesign and Data migration
    2. Dashboard redesign
    3. Cartographic style guides and themes

    1. Survey form redesign and Data migration

    We switched to an open-source web-based application called Kobo Toolbox for defining and deploying survey forms and it's android app which also works offline for data collection. Surveys were redesigned to incorporate form logic that includes validating and restricting responses and types, conditional questions, setting defaults and cascading lists.

    The data collection was segregated into the following surveys:

    1. Rapid Infrastructure Mapping – mapping of existing infrastructure of slum
    2. Rapid Infrastructure factsheet – profiling of slum
    3. Rapid Household Surveys – collecting household level data
    4. Individual factsheet – for maintaining record of completed toilets

    The process involved charting elaborate decision trees on MindMeister for each of the surveys and defining them on Kobo Form Builder as per the logic developed with user research.

    All the existing data in multiple formats was migrated to the newly created forms. This migrated data then fed into the dashboards designed for different users.

    2. Dashboard redesign

    Goals

    1. The dashboard for various users would be a unified form of access for all the data that Shelter collects.
    2. It must allow the user to zero-in on specific data points and further inspect it for deeper insights.
    3. It must answer frequently asked questions at a glance.
